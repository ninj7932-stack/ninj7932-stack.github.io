Rank: O2 First Lieutenat

Status: ACTIVE

LORE: Pixandra Elliot was born to a normal Russian family residing in Herefordshire, England. She grew up in a humble household with loving parents, her father employed as a bank employee, her mother a homemaker. By all civilian records, her life was stable, and quiet At the age of eleven, tragedy struck. The local jurisdiction had long been suspected of corruption by multiple intelligence entities. Tensions escalated into an armed confrontation within Pixandra’s neighborhood. During this incident, her parents were killed while returning from a routine trip to the mall, caught in a firefight they had no involvement in. No justice followed.The case was quietly dismissed. Charges were dropped under the justification that pursuing accountability would “create national complications.” The officers involved were never prosecuted. Pixandra was left confused, enraged, and without direction. With no immediate guardianship solution, the government transferred her into a military boarding institution under the guise of rehabilitation and structured care. The environment was brutal and unforgiving, but it taught her how to fight, endure, and survive. Discipline replaced grief. Control replaced chaos. She excelled. Pixandra later advanced into sniper training, graduating ahead of schedule. Rather than returning to civilian life, she chose to continue her military career and enlisted with a UNGOC unit, serving as a guard detail operative. Her service there was brief. While performing routine archival duties, Pixandra encountered an old, heavily redacted file. Against protocol, she pursued it further. What she uncovered was the history of a now-disbanded splinter organization once known as “The New Order” (TNO). The group’s ideology, goals, and methods resonated deeply with her own beliefs. However, the file concluded with a dead end—TNO had been officially dissolved. No surviving branches. No traceable members. Except for one name listed at the end of the document: Polaris Inc. With nothing else to follow, Pixandra began tracking the company independently. Her investigation did not go unnoticed. Instead of being silenced, she was contacted. She was offered work. Initially, Pixandra operated as a freelance agent under Polaris Inc.—deniable, unattributed, and expendable. Over time, her effectiveness drew the attention of another group seeking external support: the Order of the White Lotus (OWL). She was deployed to them under the role of a Masquerade operative. After working for a while with them, She realised that The Order of the White Lotus was the ideological successor and the more civilized and controlled version to The New Order. She stayed. Her performance as a Masquerade asset exceeded expectations. Precision, restraint, and absolute commitment defined her operations. DOSSIER: KRUNE SOLI

Rank: O4 Major

Status: ACTIVE

Lore: Krune Soli was born in Kherson, Ukraine, in 19██. His childhood was not one of wealth, but it was a childhood full of memories, like the scent of motor oil on his father’s clothes when he returned from the shipyard, or the stacks of old books in his mother’s classroom that she often brought home, or even the long evenings when the family would sit together on the balcony, watching the slow flow of the Dnipro River. Their apartment was small, with peeling wallpaper and a leaky radiator, but it was filled with warmth and charm. His father would tell him, “We don’t have much, but we keep what we do have alive.” That sense of responsibility, of holding onto the fragile things, stuck with Krune. As the eldest of three children, Krune naturally became protective. He usually walked his younger siblings, Danylo and Mira, to school.. Teachers noticed his tendency to take the blame when Mira caused trouble. This was his first taste of sacrifice: giving pieces of himself to shield others. Despite their poverty, Oksana, his mother, gave her children a world much larger than Kherson’s crumbling industrial sector. She loved folklore, particularly the old agrarian gods of pre-Christian Slavic mythology. While other children were told fairy tales, Krune grew up hearing stories of horned monsters, pasture spirits, and gods who walked with hooves through the fields. She told them not with fear, but reverence - myth as memory, not superstition. Krune was fascinated by these stories but never superstitious. To him, the stories were just fragments of so called "history". Curiosities that connected them to their land and ancestors. Little did he know they would soon become the foundation of his transformation. One night, he awoke to the smell of smoke and the sound of low chanting drifting up from the stairwell. Confused, he woke up Danylo, thinking maybe the neighbours were drunk again. But when he opened the door, he saw figures descending into the basement. They were masked, cloaked and they were carrying bowls of ash. Moments later, fire erupted in the lower floors. Whether by accident or design, the building went up in flames. Krune tried desperately to drag his siblings out, but a falling beam separated him from them. His father screamed for him to go, his mother clutching Mira and Danylo in the thick smoke. Andriy’s last command to him was not a plea for help, but a shout: “Run, Krune!” He escaped by forcing open a window onto the fire escape. When he turned back, the flames had consumed everything. The official record called it a gas leak explosion. The papers reported a tragedy. But Krune, coughing soot, eyes burning, remembered what he had seen: the masks, the horns, the chanting. And from that day, grief hardened into obsession. For months after the fire, Krune lived in a kind of half-existence. He drifted between shelters, got into fights with older teens, and stole food. He had no family, no future, and no one who would believe his story. It was during this time that he was noticed. A Global Occult Coalition recruiter, working through one of their European informant networks, learned of the boy who insisted a masked cult had killed his family. Instead of dismissing him, they conducted an investigation. What they found disturbed them: the Soli home was, in fact, the site of a ritual. Krune was given a choice: disappear into an orphanage, or put his rage and his sharp memory to use. He chose the latter. At sixteen, he was introduced into GOC’s periphery, not as an agent, but as an asset. His ability to recall faces, locations, and ritual details made him a valuable asset. Over time, as he grew older and became stronger, the GOC formalized his training. He became skilled at surveillance, field analysis, and cult infiltration. His strength wasn’t brute force, but patience and adaptability.
